Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions (SN1 & SN2)

April 20, 2026 · ~5 min read · SN1SN2Stereochemistry

Overview

Nucleophilic substitution reactions involve the replacement of a leaving group by a nucleophile. There are two main mechanisms: SN1 (unimolecular) and SN2 (bimolecular), differing in their rate laws, stereochemistry, and preferred substrates.

Key concept: The "1" and "2" refer to the kinetic order of the rate-determining step — not the number of steps in the mechanism.

SN1 Mechanism

A two-step mechanism. The rate depends only on the substrate concentration.

Rate = k[substrate]

Key Features

  • Proceeds through a carbocation intermediate
  • Favoured by tertiary substrates
  • Results in racemisation
  • Favoured by polar protic solvents (water, ethanol)
  • Weak nucleophile is sufficient

SN2 Mechanism

A concerted, one-step mechanism. Rate depends on both substrate and nucleophile.

Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile]

Key Features

  • No intermediate — bond breaking and forming occur simultaneously
  • Favoured by primary substrates
  • Results in inversion of configuration (Walden inversion)
  • Favoured by polar aprotic solvents (DMSO, acetone)
  • Strong, unhindered nucleophile required

Exam Tips

  • Identify substrate first: primary, secondary, or tertiary?
  • Check solvent: protic → SN1 tendency; aprotic → SN2 tendency
  • Strong nucleophile + polar aprotic solvent = almost always SN2
  • SN2 at a chiral centre = always inversion, never retention
  • Secondary substrates can go either way — look for other clues
Memory trick: SN2 = 2 reactants in the rate law = backside attack = inversion.
