Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions (SN1 & SN2)

April 20, 2026 · ~5 min read · Reactions Mechanisms Stereochemistry

Overview

Nucleophilic substitution reactions involve the replacement of a leaving group by a nucleophile. There are two main mechanisms: SN1 (unimolecular) and SN2 (bimolecular), which differ in their rate-determining steps, stereochemical outcomes, and preferred substrates.

Key concept: The "1" and "2" in SN1/SN2 refer to the kinetic order of the rate-determining step — not the number of steps.

SN1 Mechanism

The SN1 reaction proceeds via a two-step mechanism. The rate depends only on the concentration of the substrate.

Rate = k[substrate]

Key Features

  • Proceeds through a carbocation intermediate
  • Favoured by tertiary substrates (more stable carbocations)
  • Results in racemisation (loss of stereochemistry)
  • Favoured by polar protic solvents (e.g. water, ethanol)
  • Weak nucleophile is sufficient

SN2 Mechanism

The SN2 reaction is a concerted, one-step mechanism. The rate depends on both the substrate and the nucleophile.

Rate = k[substrate][nucleophile]

Key Features

  • No intermediate — bond breaking and forming occur simultaneously
  • Favoured by primary substrates (less steric hindrance)
  • Results in inversion of configuration (Walden inversion)
  • Favoured by polar aprotic solvents (e.g. DMSO, acetone)
  • Strong, unhindered nucleophile required

Exam Tips

  • Always identify the substrate first — primary, secondary, or tertiary?
  • Check the solvent: protic → SN1; aprotic → SN2
  • A strong nucleophile in a polar aprotic solvent almost always means SN2
  • Remember: SN2 at a chiral centre = always inversion (not retention!)
  • Secondary substrates can go either way — look for other clues
Memory trick: SN2 = 2 things in the rate law = backside attack = inversion.
